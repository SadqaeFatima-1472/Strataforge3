THE PROBLEM

Most AI features shipped in the last 18 months were never actually audited.

They were built fast, by a small team, against a deadline. Nobody went back afterward and asked what happens when the data source changes, when usage spikes, or when the model gets something confidently wrong in front of a customer.

That gap doesn't show up in a demo. It shows up in a security questionnaire you can't answer, a board member asking if the AI is safe, or an outage with no clear owner.

THE METHODOLOGY

Four risk surfaces. Every system, every time.

The same framework applied consistently, so findings are comparable and nothing gets missed because it wasn't the obvious place to look.

01

Data exposure

What the AI layer can read and write, whether access matches intent, and whether permissions have drifted since launch.

02

Architecture brittleness

Single points of failure, vendor lock-in, undocumented dependencies, and what breaks if one person leaves.

03

Output reliability

Where a wrong answer reaches a customer, what it costs if it's wrong, and whether anyone would notice first.

04

Operational readiness

Headroom at 10x load, clear ownership, and whether there's a rollback path if a change makes things worse.

SCORING

Every finding ranked the same way.

Severity and likelihood, scored independently, so a fix gets prioritized by business impact — not by which problem happens to be the most interesting technically.

Standing read access to the full customer table

5 × 4 = 20Critical
DATA EXPOSURE

The support assistant has unrestricted read access to the customer database, not just the fields it needs to answer tickets. Access was never scoped down after the prototype stage, and no one currently owns reviewing it.

Recommended fix: Scope the service account to a read-only view of ticket-relevant fields; add quarterly access review.

No rollback path for prompt changes

4 × 4 = 16Critical
OPERATIONAL READINESS

A prompt update that degraded answer quality was live for nine days before anyone noticed — there was no versioning or fast revert path, and no monitoring flagged the drop in resolution rate.

Recommended fix: Version prompts in source control; add a same-day rollback path and a weekly resolution-rate check.

Single undocumented retrieval script

3 × 4 = 12High
ARCHITECTURE BRITTLENESS

The entire retrieval pipeline depends on one script written by a former contractor. No one currently on the team has modified it or could explain several of its assumptions.

Recommended fix: Document the script's logic and dependencies; assign a current owner before any further changes.

No measured error rate on generated responses

4 × 3 = 12High
OUTPUT RELIABILITY

Generated responses reach customers with no human review step and no logged accuracy rate. The team learned about a wrong pricing answer from a customer complaint, not from monitoring.

Recommended fix: Sample and score 5% of responses weekly; route low-confidence answers to human review.

Cost scales unpredictably under load

2 × 3 = 6Watch
OPERATIONAL READINESS

Per-ticket model cost is not currently tracked or capped, so a spike in ticket volume would translate directly into an unbudgeted cost spike with no early warning.

Recommended fix: Add per-day cost tracking and an alert threshold; revisit at next audit.
